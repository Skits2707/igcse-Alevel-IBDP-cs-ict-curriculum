Section 03 — My Practice

My Practice

These aren't just Computer Science stories. They're evidence of who I am as an educator.

Story 01

The Girl Nobody Saw

Background: A shy, quiet student who hid in the classroom, barely spoke, and had no confidence. Her parents and previous teachers had given up on her academically. At age three, the verdict was already set: she wouldn't achieve anything.

My Approach: I stopped trying to 'teach' her and started trying to know her. Built trust slowly. Was honest with her — learning is hard, but we'll try. Created a space where she felt safe enough to fail and try again.

The Outcome: At four, her English skyrocketed. By five and a half, she applied those strategies to Chinese and maths. At six, she became one of the top students in her class — not because she was the most gifted, but because she knew to ask for help and believed she could. Now seven, she attends one of Hong Kong's top schools. She messages me, calls me, asks for help, shares her victories.

Relationship precedes learning. You cannot reach a child you do not see.
Story 02

The Boy Everyone Gave Up On

Background: A student with severe behavioural needs, removed from the classroom for three years. His record: screaming, kicking, scratching, throwing chairs. Labelled 'unteachable'. He wasn't even allowed to hold a pencil — too dangerous, they believed.

My Approach: I didn't fix him — he did that himself. I simply refused to give up on him. Six months of consistent expectations, patience, and finding things that made him feel capable. Built routines he could trust. Celebrated tiny wins until they became momentum.

The Outcome: He can now sit, listen, participate, and complete work independently. He wrote a story on his own — came to me beaming with pride, high-fived me, and spent a week telling everyone how proud he was. He not only started primary school — he interviewed well.

'Unteachable' usually means 'not yet reached'. Every child has a door to dignity.

Section 05 — Bilingual Practice

Bilingual Practice

EAL scaffolding in technical subjects. Every student deserves access to the language of Computer Science.

Teaching Computer Science in a bilingual context isn't just translation — it's about building conceptual bridges. Every technical term is a potential barrier for EAL learners. My approach removes those barriers systematically.

My Approach

Bilingual Keywords First — Every lesson opens with 3–5 key terms displayed in English and Chinese. Students see the vocabulary before they encounter the concept, priming comprehension.

Visual Scaffolding — Diagrams, flowcharts, and physical demonstrations reduce language dependency. A student can understand binary addition by moving physical cards before reading a single sentence.

Sentence Starters — For extended written answers, I provide sentence starters in both languages. This removes the 'blank page' anxiety and lets students focus on demonstrating technical understanding.

Collaborative Learning

Pair Before Individual — Students discuss concepts in pairs (often mixing language abilities) before individual work. This builds confidence and generates peer explanations that are sometimes more effective than the teacher's.

Bilingual Glossary Preview — Every programme includes a comprehensive glossary with English, Chinese characters, and Pinyin. Students reference these throughout the course.

Digital literacy to advanced productivity. Nine-year spiral from Grade 2 through IGCSE ICT — word processing, spreadsheets, databases, web design.

Advanced theory, programming project, and examination mastery. AS + A2 covering databases, Boolean logic, AI, and practical software development.
